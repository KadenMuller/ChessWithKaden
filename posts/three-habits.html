Three Habits That Actually Raised My Rating

Training June 21, 2026 · 3 min read

Everyone wants the shortcut — the one opening, the one course, the one trick that adds 200 points overnight. I chased plenty of those. None of them worked. What actually moved my rating was boring, repeatable, and free. Here are the three habits that did the heavy lifting.

1. Analyse every loss before you play again

It's tempting to lose a game and immediately queue up another to "win it back." That's how you lose three more. Now I make a rule: no new game until I've found the move where the last one actually went wrong — and not with the engine first, but with my own eyes.

The engine confirms or corrects me afterward. But the act of looking for the mistake myself is what trains judgment. Tilt fades, and the same blunder stops repeating.

You don't learn from losing. You learn from understanding why you lost.

2. A small dose of tactics, every single day

Not marathon sessions. Fifteen to twenty minutes of puzzles, daily, beats a three-hour grind once a week every time. Consistency keeps pattern recognition warm, so in a real game the tactics jump out instead of hiding.

The key change: I stopped racing for puzzle rating and started solving slowly, calculating the full line before moving — exactly like I should over the board.

3. Pick one opening and go deep

I used to play a different opening every week, chasing novelty. I understood none of them. Now I play a small, stable repertoire and study the resulting middlegames instead of memorising more first moves.

Knowing the plans, the typical pawn breaks, and the standard endgames of one structure is worth more than a shallow grasp of ten.

The pattern

Notice what these have in common: none is exciting, all are repeatable, and every one is about depth over novelty. That's the whole secret. Progress isn't a trick you discover — it's a habit you keep.
